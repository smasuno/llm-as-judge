## Executive Summary

Based on a systematic assessment of 29 scored signals across eight risk categories, KDDI Corporation is assigned a **Company-Specific Risk Premium (CSRP) of 100–150 basis points, with a recommended midpoint of 125 bps**, corresponding to a composite score of **2.18 out of 5.0**. This places KDDI in the lower-to-moderate risk tier relative to sector peers, reflecting the fundamental defensiveness of its business model alongside a discrete set of structural pressures that prevent a lower premium assignment. The overall risk profile is anchored by KDDI's highly predictable, subscription-driven revenue base — 「サービス提供時点で定額料金及び従量課金に基づき認識」 — which generates stable, recurring cash flows evidenced by five consecutive years of uninterrupted revenue growth from ¥5.31 trillion (FY2021) to ¥5.92 trillion (FY2025), with no cyclical drawdowns. Financial risk is materially below average: EBITDA interest coverage of approximately 102x, a net pension surplus of ¥6,179 million, cash and equivalents of ¥921 billion growing year-over-year, and negligible off-balance-sheet liabilities of only ~¥9 billion. Governance quality is above the Japanese corporate norm, supported by a board with ~55% outside directors and a transparent, orderly CEO succession completed in April 2025 — 「2025年４月当社代表取締役社長CEO（現在に至る）」 — while PwC Japan's clean, unqualified audit opinion under IFRS provides additional earnings quality assurance. Offsetting these strengths, the Operations and Market categories are the primary risk elevators: above-average capital expenditure intensity (~6.8% of revenue) driven by ongoing 5G network rollout, explicit operating leverage risk from simultaneous revenue pressure and cost escalation — 「競争激化に伴う料金値下げによる通信料収入の低下、販売コミッションやお客さま維持コストの増大」 — and a competitively intense domestic market where NTT Docomo, SoftBank, and MVNO entrants, combined with government-mandated pricing pressure, constrain pricing power. Commodity input cost exposure is broader than typical for a pure-play telecom, encompassing fuel costs, energy procurement for KDDI's electricity business, and US tariff-driven device price inflation — 「燃料高騰等による通信設備コストの増加及びエネルギー事業における電力調達コストの増加・米国による関税率の引き上げに伴う端末価格等の高騰」 — collectively justifying a premium above the minimum threshold for investment-grade integrated carriers.

### JP_04 | Board Composition and Independence | Score: 2 | Below Average Risk

**Value:** 6 of 11 directors are outside directors (~55%); Audit & Supervisory Board structure retained; Nomination Advisory Committee and Compensation Advisory Committee established.

KDDI's board governance is above average for a Japanese listed company of its scale. The filing confirms: 「取締役会においては社内取締役と社外取締役が半数ずつ選任されており、過半数の社外監査役を含む監査役会等と連携する体制を採用しています」 — outside directors constitute a bare majority of the board, and the Audit & Supervisory Board itself includes a majority of outside statutory auditors, creating a dual layer of independent oversight. The establishment of independent-chaired Nomination Advisory and Compensation Advisory Committees further aligns KDDI's governance architecture with Japan's Corporate Governance Code best-practice recommendations and represents a meaningful step beyond the minimum compliance threshold observed among many Japanese mid-cap issuers.

However, two structural limitations moderate the score from the lowest risk tier:

- **Retention of the Audit & Supervisory Board (監査役会設置会社) model:** KDDI has not transitioned to the Three-Committee Company (三委員会設置会社) or Audit & Supervisory Committee Company (監査等委員会設置会社) structures, which would vest formal oversight authority more directly in the board itself. The traditional 監査役会 model, while legally compliant, provides somewhat less integrated board-level accountability than the alternative structures increasingly favoured by institutional investors and proxy advisors in Japan.

- **Low insider equity ownership:** As noted in MGMT_04, total officer shareholdings of 「計 184,400」 shares are negligible relative to total shares outstanding, which is characteristic of large Japanese corporates but limits the degree to which board and management incentives are directly aligned with shareholder value creation — a persistent structural feature of Japanese corporate governance that an acquirer should factor into post-transaction integration planning.

Nonetheless, the following observations are relevant for a complete Japan-specific risk picture:

- **政策保有株式 (Strategic Cross-Shareholdings):** The filing discloses commercial relationships between KDDI and affiliates of its outside directors, including Kyocera Corporation and Toyota Motor Corporation — 「当社と京セラ株式会社及びトヨタ自動車株式会社の間には商取引関係がありますが、その取引額はいずれも双方から見て売上高の５％未満です」. The filing acknowledges: 「当社の必要に応じた周波数を獲得できるかどうか」 — spectrum allocation dependency creates an implicit ongoing relationship with the regulatory authority that, while standard for the sector, represents a form of quasi-governmental influence on strategic decision-making that is distinct from typical commercial market risk and should be considered in any control transaction scenario.

## Top 3 Risk Drivers

The three signals below received the highest individual scores in the assessment and are the primary factors preventing KDDI from achieving a lower CSRP. Each represents a structural or competitive pressure that an acquirer or investor should monitor as a priority risk in any valuation or transaction context.

### Risk Driver #1 | OPS_04 — Operating Leverage | Score: 4

**Category:** Operations
**Value:** Simultaneous revenue pressure from regulatory price competition and cost escalation from network construction, new frequency band deployment, and customer retention expenditure.

KDDI faces a particularly adverse combination of top-line and cost-side pressures that compress operating margins in a manner that is difficult to offset through volume growth alone. On the revenue side, government-driven pricing reform in Japan's mobile market has structurally reduced average revenue per user, while intensifying competition from NTT Docomo, SoftBank, and MVNO entrants further limits pricing recovery. On the cost side, the ongoing 5G rollout requires simultaneous investment in new base station infrastructure and maintenance of legacy networks, while customer acquisition and retention costs continue to rise. The filing explicitly captures this dual pressure: 「競争激化に伴う料金値下げによる通信料収入の低下、販売コミッションやお客さま維持コストの増大」 — declining mobile service revenue from price competition and growing sales commission and customer retention costs are identified as explicit risk factors in the same disclosure passage.

The risk is compounded by the largely fixed-cost nature of telecom network infrastructure: once capital is committed to base station construction and spectrum deployment, the cost base is largely inflexible in the near term, meaning that any revenue shortfall from accelerated price competition or subscriber churn flows disproportionately through to operating income. This operating leverage dynamic is the single most significant idiosyncratic risk factor in KDDI's profile and is the primary reason the Operations category scores 3.50 — the highest category score in the assessment.

**Monitoring Indicators:** ARPU trend in the Personal Business segment; sales commission expense as a percentage of mobile service revenue; operating income margin trajectory in quarterly filings.

### Risk Driver #2 | MKT_02 — Competitive Intensity | Score: 4

**Category:** Market Position
**Value:** Explicit price competition risk from NTT Docomo, SoftBank, MNO/MVNO new entrants, and cross-industry competitors offering bundled services.

Japan's mobile telecommunications market is characterised by government-mandated pricing pressure that has structurally altered the competitive dynamics for all three major MNOs. KDDI's filing identifies a broad and escalating competitive threat landscape that extends well beyond traditional carrier-to-carrier rivalry: 「異業種との提携、通信と電力等のその他商品とのセット販売、MNO、MVNO事業者の新規参入、他事業者の事業領域の拡大等の事業環境の変化に伴う競争の激化」 — competition is intensifying through cross-industry alliances, bundled offerings combining telecommunications with electricity and other products, new MNO and MVNO entrants, and the expansion of existing operators into adjacent business domains.

This disclosure is notable for its breadth: the competitive threat is not limited to traditional mobile pricing wars but encompasses the structural blurring of industry boundaries as technology, energy, and financial services companies increasingly compete for the same customer wallet. KDDI's own diversification into electricity and financial services — while a strategic response to this dynamic — simultaneously exposes it to competitive pressure in those adjacent markets, as the filing also acknowledges: 「金融事業における競争において期待通りの収入を上げられるかどうか」. The NTT group's ongoing consolidation and potential regulatory advantages further amplify the competitive risk for KDDI specifically, as NTT's infrastructure ownership creates asymmetric cost advantages in fixed-mobile convergence that KDDI must continuously invest to offset.

The partial mitigant — meaningful regulatory barriers to entry under the Telecommunications Business Act and Radio Act spectrum licensing (MKT_03, score: 2) — limits the risk of entirely new infrastructure-based entrants but does not address the pricing and bundling pressure from existing well-capitalised competitors.

**Monitoring Indicators:** Mobile subscriber net additions and churn rate; sub-brand (povo) subscriber trajectory; market share data published by the Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications.

### Risk Driver #3 | MKT_05 — Commodity and Input Cost Exposure | Score: 4

**Category:** Market Position
**Value:** Fuel cost increases for network equipment; energy procurement cost increases in the electricity business; US tariff-driven device price inflation; domestic labour, construction, and general price inflation.

KDDI's input cost exposure is materially broader than that of a pure-play mobile carrier, reflecting the diversified nature of its business portfolio. The filing enumerates an unusually wide set of cost pressures in a single risk disclosure: 「燃料高騰等による通信設備コストの増加及びエネルギー事業における電力調達コストの増加・日本国内における人件費や建設費、物価の高騰に伴う仕入コスト等の増加・米国による関税率の引き上げに伴う端末価格等の高騰」 — covering fuel-driven network equipment cost increases, electricity procurement cost increases in the energy business segment, domestic labour and construction cost inflation, and US tariff-driven device price escalation.

Three dimensions of this risk are particularly notable in the current macroeconomic environment. First, Japan's energy cost environment remains elevated following the structural repricing of global energy markets, and KDDI's dual exposure — as both a consumer of energy for network operations and a retailer of electricity to end customers — creates a two-sided margin squeeze that pure-play telecoms do not face. Second, US tariff escalation on imported devices (primarily smartphones manufactured in Asia) threatens to raise handset prices in Japan, potentially dampening upgrade cycles and reducing device-linked revenue and commission income. Third, Japan's domestic construction and labour cost inflation, driven by a tight labour market and materials cost increases, directly affects the cost of ongoing 5G base station rollout — compounding the capex intensity risk already identified in OPS_03 (score: 4). Together, these input cost vectors create a cost environment that is structurally more challenging than the headline telecom sector average, and their simultaneous occurrence limits KDDI's ability to manage any single cost line through operational hedging or procurement optimisation alone.

**Monitoring Indicators:** Energy procurement cost disclosures in segment reporting; handset average selling price trends; construction cost indices in Japan; gross margin trajectory in the Business segment.

### Limitations and Recommended Supplementary Work

Users of this report should note the following limitations inherent to the current assessment:

1. **Filing scope:** The analysis is based solely on the filing sections provided. A full annual securities report (有価証券報告書) review, including the complete notes to financial statements, the detailed risk factor section, and the corporate governance report (コーポレート・ガバナンス報告書), would likely resolve several of the unscored signals and could cause the composite score to move modestly in either direction.

2. **Comparables mode off:** Because no peer benchmarking was performed, the sector-average anchors used for peer-average scores (score: 3) reflect the model's trained priors for Japanese integrated telecommunications carriers rather than a live peer group analysis. Activating comparables mode with a defined peer set (e.g., NTT, SoftBank Group, Rakuten Mobile) would provide a more precise relative risk calibration.

3. **Point-in-time assessment:** This report reflects conditions as of the run date of **2026-06-10** and the filing data available at that time. KDDI's risk profile — particularly in the Operations and Market categories, where the highest scores were recorded — is sensitive to regulatory developments in Japan's mobile market, 5G rollout progress, and macroeconomic input cost dynamics that may evolve materially between the assessment date and the closing of any transaction in which this report is used.

4. **Japan-specific supplemental flags:** The JP_04 signal and the contextual observations regarding 政策保有株式 and メインバンク relationships are excluded from the weighted composite by design. An acquirer conducting a control transaction should independently assess the scale of KDDI's cross-shareholding portfolio and any associated unwinding obligations, as these could have material implications for post-acquisition capital structure and governance that are not reflected in the 125 bps midpoint CSRP.
